*(This is going to be a longer rant, but bear with me.)*

Imagine this: You are on sick leave, in bed. Your phone rings. Once, twice. You decide to take it though you do not recognise the number - and it is through snot and headaches that you come to understand it's a sales person from a famous SEO tool provider. You tell them you are off sick and that you do not appreciate being called on your private phone number, and hope that's the end of it.
You have no idea how they got your private phone number but it was likely when you spoke at a conference and allowed that your number was given to companies at the exhibition. This usually gets you great deals, eg. testing a new tool for free for 30 days. Not this time. This time, that decision is going to be one you'll regret.

<!--more-->

 The next time they reach out, you are in a meeting, presenting your new strategy to stakeholders. Your phone rings. You ignore it. It rings again. You look at the screen: a mobile phone number with your country ID, not flagged as spam. You cannot take it right now, so you refuse the call. It immediately dials again - and again, and again, until you have to switch your phone off, rendering yourself unreachable (what if your family had an emergency?), just so you can continue with your call. 

This continues to happen over the next few days. Eventually, you decide to block the number. But what's this? Within less than 5 seconds, you get another call, this time from a different number. A different, neighbouring country even. You block that one too - but this continues until, again, you switch your phone off.

Silence for a few days. If you think they finally got the message - you are mistaken. They will come at you again. This time, you decide to speak. The sales person is nice. They got a charming British accent and you know how hard their job is. So you decide to be upfront, telling them upfront that you see no value in spending an hour in your busy work day for a demo call with someone who won't be able to answer in-depth questions, for a tool at the cost of somewhere in between 50k and 80k a year, hors taxes.

I get why they might think I'd be a good client:
I work in a large enterprise, so we have budget smaller companies don't. And in a lot of enterprises, those in charge of my area of expertise aren't actually the experts, or lead large teams so they don't have time to dig into the details. They need something that helps them to justify asking for resources and budget within a few clicks. Something that produces pretty dashboards. But that is just not me. To understand how something works, I might just build it myself to have a proof of concept - and that makes me a difficult customer. If you cannot show me the guts of the tool you're selling, and help me tear them apart and take a good look, we're not going to work together. 

The question I had? 
"How do you track those LLM prompts that you are trying to sell? How do you generate them to come closer to what real users might be looking for?"

{{< highlight label="the bigger picture" >}}
You see, with the rising popularity of chatbots and Google's search results getting worse, everyone and their parents are trying to be the first who found the secret sauce to tracking what users are seeing in LLMs. We are back in the very early stages of SEO, pre-"webmaster tools" era. It is as exciting as it's frustrating because we keep making the same mistakes we should have outgrown by now.

That is an important question that I'll be tackling in a different article. For now, the one thing we need to be aware of is that the more tools we all use to track made-up aka synthetic prompts are polluting the one source of truth we might have had: access logs - which you might use for the very question your CMO asks you: how many times have we been seen by users in LLMs? And before you know it, you'll base your expensive decisions on how many other SEO teams have been sending made-up prompts into the nether.
{{< /highlight >}}

How did the story continue?
The sales person was unable to answer my question but offered:
"If you send me an email repeating what you just asked, I'll forward it to our tech team and will get you someone from their team for that call instead of a generic demo call. Sound good?"

Dear reader, my curiosity is the very thing that always gets me into trouble: of course I said yes. That call, however, was cancelled and moved by them last minute many times, and when we finally had it - it was, you might have guessed it, just a generic demo call. The poor person trying their best to show me pretty (basic) dashboards in an overpriced SEO suite was unable to answer my question and had never even heard about the promise their sales colleague had made.

And so I kept being harrassed by calls, apologies, and offers to really make it right this time for months. By different numbers, and at odd times of the day, despite me saying I had lost all interest in the matter for good.

------

I will say this: Kudos to your sales people, and whoever trained them. They know how to be apologetic, and then throw in just the right crumbs to relieve someone's anger, and to get you just that tiny bit interested to stay in the line for five more minutes, during which they will use manipulation tactics to make you agree to, eventually, have another call.

Just that today, I finally had it, and told them to delete my number from their system once and for all (and for real this time; I am sure I did that before - please, dear Sales people, be reminded that as per German law, what you have been doing is illegal, and that my lawyer might enjoy getting some work).

Another thing to keep in mind, dear CMOs running Enterprise SaaS Sales teams:
The people you have your team calling are likely busy, and they need to justify the hefty cost if they were to request budget for your tool. If they really were interested, it would help your case if you made their job really easy for them. Take the load off their shoulders for budget requests. Figure out what the best argument will be within their company.

That's not done with a one-size-fits-all superficial demo call. At best, it wastes your time and theirs. At worst, it makes someone who could have been interested, who might have had the budget, and who literally told you what they need to know to make their decision, having to block you and inform everyone in their company AND in their network to do the same, harming your brand as a result.

The pressure to increase revenue makes you use shady call centre tech and tactics that those you want to sell to cannot distinguish from those robot spam calls. If you make them despise their phone, they will despise those who cause that feeling - and that will be your brand.
While I love a good rant (if you publish rants, send them my way, I love reading them even more than writing them myself!), I also believe in kindness. So while I kindly told your sales person to forget my existence, I won't name the brand.
