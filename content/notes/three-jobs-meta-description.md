Still relevant. Still misunderstood. Updated thinking on what snippet copy actually does, mechanically, in a world where Google rewrites it 60% of the time anyway.

**Job one: click-through rate.** This is the obvious one, and the one most practitioners over-index on. A well-written meta description can improve CTR. But Google rewrites descriptions so frequently now that your carefully crafted 155 characters are often replaced with content it thinks better matches the query. Write the description anyway, but don't treat it as your primary CTR lever.

**Job two: intent signalling.** The meta description tells Google what you think your page is about and who it's for. Even when Google rewrites the visible snippet, it uses your description as one input into its understanding of page relevance. A description that accurately signals intent helps with ranking even if users never see it.

**Job three: AI context.** This is the new one. Language models that process web pages often weight meta descriptions as high-signal summaries. Your description may appear in AI-generated answers, training data, or retrieval contexts where the full page content isn't available. Writing a clear, factual description that stands alone as a summary is more important now than it's ever been - not for Google's SERP, but for every other system that reads your page.

Three jobs. One tag. Most people are only optimising for the first one, which is increasingly the one they have the least control over.
