The argument is simple and worth stating plainly: AI systems trained to avoid controversy don't just avoid controversy - they actively model a kind of intellectual cowardice that normalises hedging as default behaviour. Every "on the one hand / on the other hand" non-answer teaches readers that ambiguity is acceptable where clarity is possible.

What I find interesting is the SEO parallel. **Hedged content ranks.** It ranks because it matches the intent signals of users who are themselves uncertain. We've been inadvertently training search systems to reward exactly the same epistemic posture the author is criticising in AI.

The piece doesn't go here - it's more focused on the user experience of AI hedging - but I think the structural problem is the same. Optimising for the absence of harm is not the same as optimising for the presence of value. And both search and AI have quietly confused the two.
