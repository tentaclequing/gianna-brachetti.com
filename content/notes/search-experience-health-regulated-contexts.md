Someone searches "ibuprofen stomach pain." Are they researching a side effect, or trying to buy something for their stomach pain, or worried they've taken too much? The intent is ambiguous, and the stakes of guessing wrong are not measured in bounce rates.

I spent years in and around health content - medical information portals, online pharmacies, biopharma equipment. Half my family works in healthcare. I've proofread papers on nephrology outcomes and written SOPs for hospital procurement workflows. The thing that sticks with me from all of it: in health, the cost of bad search results is not "user didn't click." It's "user made a medical decision based on what a ranking algorithm surfaced."

Most search experience work treats trust signals as a conversion problem. Schema markup, review stars, authority badges. In health contexts, trust signals are a safety mechanism. The E-E-A-T framework exists because Google got burned by content farms ranking for cancer treatment queries. That's not an abstraction. People followed bad advice.

The tension I keep returning to: informational and transactional intent in health search are tangled in ways they aren't elsewhere. "Metformin weight loss" is simultaneously a clinical question, a purchase intent, and sometimes a cry for help from someone whose doctor won't listen to them. No amount of keyword clustering resolves that. It requires product thinking about what the search experience owes the person behind the query.

Regulation shapes this more than most search practitioners acknowledge. An online pharmacy can't just "optimise for conversions" the way a shoe shop can. Prescription requirements, advertising restrictions, mandatory patient information - these aren't obstacles to a good search experience, they're the architecture of a responsible one. I learned at arvato that the compliance layer and the UX layer are the same layer, whether you design for that or not.

What frustrates me is how rarely this gets discussed in product and search circles as a design problem. It's treated as a compliance checkbox. But the people searching for health information at 2 AM are not thinking about your regulatory framework. They need the search experience to have already thought about it for them.
