The framing of Generative Engine Optimisation as a successor discipline keeps collapsing under scrutiny. Every time I try to map it against classical search theory, the categories break down. The problem isn't that GEO is wrong - it's that it's premature. We're naming a thing before we understand its shape.

The core claim of GEO is that optimising for AI-generated answers requires fundamentally different techniques than optimising for traditional search results. But when you look at what those techniques actually are - clear structure, authoritative sourcing, entity salience, comprehensive coverage - they're just good SEO. What's changed is the rendering layer, not the underlying signals.

Where it gets genuinely interesting is at the edges. There are real differences in how language models evaluate content compared to traditional ranking algorithms. Models care about coherence across a passage in ways that keyword-frequency metrics don't capture. They're sensitive to contradiction in ways that link-based authority signals aren't. But calling that a new discipline rather than an evolution of the existing one feels like marketing dressed as methodology.
